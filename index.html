Executive assessment

Security priorities tied to player trust and service continuity

Activision’s public materials show a global, identity-heavy, always-online business that handles account information, linked platform identities, gameplay and communications data, purchase history, device information, and virtual assets. The most important risk theme is not a single technical flaw; it is the concentration of business value in consumer identity, game integrity, software delivery, and continuous availability.

  • Protect identity as a revenue and trust control.
    Player account takeover can create fraud, support cost, reputational damage, enforcement errors, and loss of virtual assets.
  • Treat the build pipeline and anti-cheat intellectual property as crown jewels.
    Compromise could affect code integrity, game fairness, release schedules, and brand confidence.
  • Measure resilience in business terms.
    Live-service disruption should be governed through service-level objectives, game-launch readiness, tested recovery, and executive escalation thresholds.
  • Unify privacy, security, and platform governance.
    The data lifecycle spans linked accounts, advertising, communications, customer support, mobile, console, PC, and third parties.

Board-level conclusion

Security should be managed as a product-quality, revenue-protection, and trust program—not only an IT control function.

The recommended operating model joins player identity, anti-fraud, anti-cheat, privacy, software delivery, infrastructure resilience, and third-party governance under a common risk taxonomy. Leadership should receive a small set of measurable indicators: privileged phishing-resistant MFA coverage, account-recovery fraud rate, signed-production-artifact coverage, KEV remediation performance, high-severity service availability, tested recovery success, and overdue privacy-retention exceptions.

Evidence quality rules

How I would avoid weak audit conclusions

  • Completeness: reconcile system-generated populations to authoritative inventories or HR records before sampling.
  • Accuracy: inspect report logic, source systems, filters, timestamps, and access controls.
  • Operating period: obtain evidence across the review period, not only a point-in-time screenshot.
  • Exception handling: trace exceptions to risk acceptance, accountable owner, compensating control, and expiry date.
  • Reperformance: independently reproduce critical calculations or configuration checks when feasible.

Tabletop scenario

Compromised build engineer and malicious release path

A cross-functional exercise covering identity, code integrity, player safety, privacy, legal, communications, and service recovery.

INJECT 01

Identity compromise

A build engineer approves a realistic phishing prompt. The attacker obtains a session token and accesses source repositories from a new device.

INJECT 02

Pipeline activity

A protected branch alert shows a dependency change and attempted access to signing infrastructure shortly before a major content release.

INJECT 03

Player impact

Telemetry identifies anomalous client behavior. Social media claims a malicious update is stealing credentials, but facts remain incomplete.

INJECT 04

Business decision

Leadership must decide whether to halt deployment, revoke artifacts, force sessions to reauthenticate, notify platforms, and communicate publicly.

Required decisions

Containment authority

Who can halt releases, revoke signing trust, disable identities, and isolate environments without waiting for a full executive meeting?

Evidence preservation

How are identity, repository, CI/CD, signing, endpoint, cloud, and game telemetry preserved with defensible timestamps?

Player protection

What threshold triggers token revocation, password reset, 2FA challenge, platform notification, or customer support surge staffing?

Disclosure analysis

Who evaluates privacy, contractual, regulatory, insurer, platform, law-enforcement, and public-communication obligations?

Recovery acceptance

What evidence proves the build environment, dependencies, signing process, and released artifacts are trustworthy before resuming?

Post-incident governance

How are root causes, control failures, risk acceptance, accountability, and tracked remediation presented to leadership?

How I work

From evidence to a defensible recommendation

The portfolio follows the same logic used in consulting and assurance work: understand the environment, define criteria, collect evidence, assess risk, and communicate remediation in business terms.

01

Understand the system

Map business services, users, data, network paths, cloud dependencies, identity flows, and failure points before judging controls.

02

Set the criteria

Choose the relevant framework outcomes, regulatory obligations, contractual expectations, and risk appetite.

03

Test with evidence

Use configurations, tickets, logs, screenshots, policies, interviews, samples, and technical outputs to distinguish design from operating effectiveness.

04

Prioritize the risk

Consider likelihood, impact, exploitability, exposure, asset criticality, compensating controls, and remediation effort.

05

Communicate the decision

Translate the issue into business impact, accountable ownership, target dates, resource needs, and measurable success criteria.
